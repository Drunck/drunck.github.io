At the beginning of XX century, livestock breeding continued to be the main type of economy in the steppe, and the Kazakh population continued to lead a nomadic way of life. The nomadic economy of the Kazakhs at the beginning of the century began to experience the impact of the Russian influence (market). A significant part of cattle-breeding farms was drawn into trade and market relations.

Expansion of contacts with the Russian market and fairs changed the structure of the herd in Kazakh farms towards an increase in the number of horses and cattle.

New forms of economy were formed: settled cattle-breeding and sedentary agricultural. In its pure form, the nomadic economy remained in the steppe regions.

In the northern regions it was already rare to find a purely nomadic economy not connected with agriculture (grain)

Although the number of Kazakhs who began to sow the land as a whole increased, agriculture for them continued to be a secondary and auxiliary branch of the nomadic economy. It can also be concluded that the agrarian policy of the Russian administration as a whole did not stimulate the development of agriculture among the Kazakhs. A new phenomenon in the nomadic economy was that ruined nomadic herders went to work in cities, Cossack villages, resettlement villages (annually up to 20 thousand people)

The most important result of the accession of Kazakhstan to Russia was the gradual pulling of the region into the all-Russian market and commodity-money relations. Kazakhstan is turning into a raw material base and a sales market for the Russian industry. The poorly developed industry of Kazakhstan was represented by mining, as well as processing and food enterprises.

At the beginning of XX century, foreign entrepreneurs began to invest in mining enterprises (Altai and Central Kazakhstan). Thus, the Anglo-French joint-stock community of Spassky copper ores, which arose in 1904 in London, acquired the Spassko-Uspensky copper mine and plant, the Saransk-Karaganda coal mines, and iron mines In Western Kazakhstan and the Ural-Embensky region, joint-stock companies were engaged in the development of oil fields in which English capital predominated ("Ural-Embensky company", "West Ural oil company", etc.)

Coal deposits (Karaganda, Ekibastuz) were actively developed, gold was mined, and the salt industry developed rapidly. Increased access to railways is a manifestation of Kazakhstan's pulling into the all-Russian market. At the beginning of the century, the construction of railways began, connecting the region with the industrial centers of Russia. The main railway at that time was the Orenburg-Tashkent road (1901-1905). Troitskaya, Altai and Semirechenskaya railways were built. Branches of the largest Russian banks are opened in Russian Commercial Industrial Bank, Russian-Asian Bank, Petersburg and others.

The main goods were imported livestock and animal products.

The development of industry and the construction of railways contributed to the formation of the working class, which in 1913 reached 75 thousand people

In 1914 Russia entered the First World War. The war caused the collapse of industry, the decline of agriculture, transport and financial crises. The situation of the population of the region has worsened. For the needs of the warriors of the population at compulsory prices, they bought up livestock, requisitioned food, yurts and felt.

The beginning of the 1916 national liberation uprising took place.

The main reasons for the uprising: increased colonial oppression, the seizure of land from the indigenous population, an increase in taxes and levies, a sharp deterioration in the situation of the Kazakh population in connection with the war.

The reason for the rebellion was the Tsar’s decree of June 25, 1916. On the call for the rear work of the Kazakh male population (from 19 to 43 years old) for the construction of defensive structures in the battle area. The compilation of the lists, entrusted to the volost governors, led to massive bribery and abuse.

At the beginning of July 1916 spontaneous uprisings began in almost all areas of the region, which grew into an armed uprising

The largest centers of the uprising were the Turgai region and Semirechye. The uprising in the Semirechensk region (leaders T. Bokin and B. Ashekeyev) began in early July. A congress/meeting of Kazakhs took place on Ushkonur tract, at which it was decided not to send people to rear work, to destroy the telegraph, and to destroy the lists of mobilized people.

To suppress the uprising, the government sent punitive troops. Large clashes with government troops took place in Kazalinsky, Aulie-Atinsky, Shymkent and Vernensky districts. In SeptemberOctober, a significant part of the Semirechye rebels, pressed from all sides by the tsarist troops, was forced to leave for Western China; some auls migrated to the Turgai region.

The uprising in the Turgai region, led by A. Imanov and A. Dzhangildin, turned out to be the most long-lasting. The number of insurgents here reached 50 thousand people. The Turgai center of the uprising was distinguished by a well-coordinated control system of the insurrectionary movement. Commander Amangeldy Imanov divided the army into tens, fifty and hundreds and thousands.

The military council (kenes) was in charge of military matters.

The most important aspect of the uprising was that the rebels restored the traditional (pre-colonial) institutions of government and ceased to obey the colonial administration. Khans were elected from representatives of the aristocracy. Thus, the uprising, which began with a protest against the actions of the tsarist government, acquired the character of an anti-colonial, national liberation war of the Kazakh people. The insurgent had to fight the superior forces of the government. By the beginning of the February Revolution, the main detachments of the rebels were defeated.

In February 1917, a revolution took place in Petrograd, overthrowing the monarchy. The weakness of Russian democracy was manifested in the fact that a dual power was established in Russia. The Provisional Government, representing the forces of democracy and capitalism, shared power with the Soviets of Workers ‘ and Soldiers’ Deputies, which became the center of concentration of pro-socialist populist forces, among which the Bolsheviks took over by the fall.

In Kazakhstan, news of the events in Petrograd caused a wave of meetings and demonstrations in support of the revolution. In the region, as well as throughout the country, a dual power was established. The authorities representing the Provisional Government were formed in March-April 1917.

The Provisional Government appointed former officials of the previous administration at the head of the regional authorities, and also attracted representatives of the Kazakh intelligentsia to the administration.

A.Bukeikhanov was appointed commissar of Turgai region, and M. Tynyshpaev - the Semirechensk region In the volosts, auls, villages, the former volost and aul governors remained. Doctrine of National Unity of Kazakhstan

was developed in order to implement the instructions of the President of the Republic of Kazakhstan given at the XIV session of the Assembly of the People of Kazakhstan on October 23, 2008 (diaspora populations from many nationalities are represented in the Assembly including, Armenians, Belarusians, Chechen-Ingush, Germans, Greeks, Koreans, Kyrgyz, Poles and Ukrainians among others)

The doctrine is based on the provisions of the Constitution, the Law "On the Assembly of the People of Kazakhstan" and other regulatory legal acts of the Republic of Kazakhstan, as well as the norms of international law in this area.

The purpose of this Doctrine is to determine the priorities and mechanisms for ensuring national unity in Kazakhstan on the basis of civic identity, patriotism, spiritual and cultural community, maintaining stability, interethnic and interfaith harmony in society.

First of all, this document proceeds from the fact that at the present stage of development of Kazakhstan, the social harmony and interethnic tolerance achieved over the years of its independence should move to a qualitatively new level - national unity, based on the recognition of a system of values and principles common to all citizens.

Main principles:

1. One country - one destiny.

2. Different origins - equal opportunities

3. Development of the national spirit

The doctrine is the basis for creating an integral system of legal social economic, political measures aimed at strengthening the unity of the peoples of Kazakhstan, the development of democracy, dialogue of cultures and civilizations

Kazakhstan and the United Nations

The Republic of Kazakhstan became a full member of the United Nations on March 2, 1992 (GA resolution 46/224). The United Nations began its work in Kazakhstan in early 1993.

Kazakhstan's accession to the United Nations meant the implementation of the age-old aspirations of the people for independence and freedom, confirmed the recognition of its contribution to the development of world civilization.

Currently, the UN is represented in Kazakhstan by:

UNDP (United Nations Development Program), UNICEF (United Nations Children's Fund), UNFPA (United Nations Population Fund), UNOD (United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime), UNHCR (Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees), UNV (UN Volunteers), ILO (International Labor Organization), UNESCO (United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization), WHO (World Health Organization), WB (World Bank), IMF (International Monetary Fund), UN / AIDS (Joint UN Program on HIV / AIDS),DPI (Office of the Department of Public Information), etc.

Relations between the Republic of Kazakhstan and the European Union, the European Community, today are based on the Partnership and Cooperation Agreement between the Republic of Kazakhstan, on the one hand, the European Communities and their member states, on the other hand, concluded on January 23, 1995 in Brussels (Belgium).

The agreement entered into force on July 1, 1999 after it passed its ratification in the Parliament of the Republic of Kazakhstan (Mazhilis), the European Parliament and in all national parliaments of the Union and Communities member states.

The objectives of this partnership are:

providing an appropriate framework for political dialogue between the Parties, contributing to the development of political relations between them;

promoting trade, investment and harmonious economic relations between the Parties, and thus encouraging sustainable economic development of the Parties;

providing a basis for economic, social, financial and cultural cooperation

supporting the efforts of the Republic of Kazakhstan to strengthen its democracy, develop its economy and complete the transition to a market economy
